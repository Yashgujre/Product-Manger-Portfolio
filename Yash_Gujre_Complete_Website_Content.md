## Project 1: Centene B2B Care Coordination Platform

**B2B Healthcare Platform · Enterprise · Managed Care**

### Overview

Embedded a secure B2B module into Centene Corporation's managed care infrastructure, enabling 50+ nonprofit community-based organizations to coordinate interventions for high-risk Medicaid members in real time. Replaced fragmented email-and-spreadsheet workflows with structured referral management, automated outcome tracking, and HIPAA-compliant audit trails. The platform processes 5,000–10,000 referrals per month across 1,000+ case workers accessing data for 2.5 million+ members.

### The Problem

Centene partners with nonprofit organizations to address social determinants of health (SDOH) for its Medicaid population — housing instability, food insecurity, behavioral health needs. But the partnership model was fundamentally broken at the information layer.

Nonprofit case workers received member referrals via email with no real-time eligibility data, no risk stratification scores, no care plans, and no feedback loops. A case worker receiving a housing referral for a member named Maria would not know whether Maria was still enrolled in Centene's plan, what her comorbidities were, what risk score Centene assigned her, whether other partners were already working with her, or what care plan existed. The case worker would spend 1.5 hours per member on manual intake research — making phone calls, searching emails, copying data between spreadsheets — before even beginning an intervention.

At 500 referrals per year per nonprofit, that translated to 750 hours of wasted labor annually per organization. Across Centene's 50+ partner network: $937,500 per year in aggregate waste. But the real cost was measured in delayed interventions. Every day a housing referral sat unacknowledged, a member with diabetes and depression cycled back through the ER. The average referral acknowledgment time was 4.2 days. First member contact averaged 6.3 days. SLA violations ran at 18%.

### My Process

I led a qualitative discovery study across 12 nonprofits in 4 states (Texas, Florida, California, New York). The research methodology included 8 in-depth interviews with nonprofit leadership and operations directors, 3 days of ride-alongs shadowing case workers in the field, process mapping sessions documenting current referral workflows end-to-end, and financial analysis estimating hidden costs of manual coordination.

The ride-alongs were transformative. I watched case workers toggle between 6 browser tabs, manually transcribe member information from Centene emails into their own spreadsheets, and make follow-up calls that went unanswered for days. One case worker told me: "I spend Monday morning digging through emails to find new referrals. By the time I understand the full picture, a week has passed."

I synthesized the discovery into a four-part problem diagnosis: fragmented information access, manual unstructured coordination, inability to measure outcomes, and compliance and security constraints. I sized the total opportunity at $4.1M–$24M annually and presented the business case to the Chief Medical Officer's office.

The critical strategic decision was building an embedded B2B module rather than a standalone SaaS platform. This ensured zero data duplication (single source of truth in Centene's care management system), centralized compliance (one audit trail, one BAA framework), and reusable architecture that could scale from 3 to 50+ nonprofits nationally without redesign.

I led 12 two-week engineering sprints from API design through phased nonprofit rollout. The API surface included 50+ pages of OpenAPI 3.0 specifications covering member context, referral workflows, task management, outcome tracking, and audit logging.

### Architecture

The system was designed as a microservices architecture sitting on top of Centene's existing care management infrastructure. The frontend used React 18 with TypeScript, optimized for field conditions with offline-capable workflows and sub-2-second load times. The backend consisted of an API Gateway handling authentication, authorization, and logging, with separate microservices for member context, referral management, task workflow, outcome tracking, and reporting. The data layer integrated directly with Centene's member database, care management system, and claims data warehouse. Security architecture included role-based access control with field-level data masking (HIPAA), full audit logging of every data access event, and TLS 1.3 encryption for all API traffic.

## Project 2: Capital One Trade Credit & Risk Decisioning Platform

**B2B Fintech Platform · Enterprise · Credit Infrastructure**

### Overview

Designed and launched an enterprise-grade trade credit infrastructure platform built by Capital One for Wayfair Professional. The platform automated real-time credit decisions at B2B checkout, replacing 2–7 day manual review cycles with sub-second API-driven approvals. Architecture was replicated across 3+ enterprise clients within 18 months of launch.

### The Problem

Wayfair Professional serves 100,000+ business customers — interior design firms, general contractors, property managers — who purchase furniture, fixtures, and equipment on credit terms (Net-30 to Net-60). The existing credit review process was entirely manual: when a new business customer applied for credit at checkout, their application was routed to a credit analyst who would pull D&B reports, review financial statements, check references, and make a decision. This process took 2–7 business days.

The impact was devastating for conversion. 15–20% of B2B orders were abandoned because buyers expected instant approval at checkout, not a multi-day wait. With Wayfair Professional's B2B gross merchandise value exceeding $1.2 billion annually, even a 10% recovery of abandoned orders represented $120M+ in revenue. Simultaneously, manual credit decisions led to inconsistent risk assessment — some analysts were too conservative (rejecting profitable customers) while others were too lenient (approving customers who later defaulted). The default rate was running higher than industry benchmarks.

I sized the total opportunity at $458M–$608M in annual revenue and loss impact: $450M+ from recovering abandoned high-value orders, $4M+ from reducing defaults through better risk scoring, $4M from improved fraud detection, and $450K from operational cost reduction.

### My Process

I began by evaluating three delivery models using a weighted scorecard: Build (in-house from scratch), Buy (off-the-shelf platforms like TreviPay, Balance, or Credit Key), and Partner (co-develop with client). Build scored low on speed (18–24 months, $15M+ in development costs). Buy fell short on customization — vendors couldn't embed Capital One's proprietary credit scoring or co-brand seamlessly. Partner scored highest (4.8/5): we could leverage Wayfair's distribution (100K+ professional buyers), share development costs, and build an API-first layer on top of Capital One's core underwriting engine.

I selected Wayfair Professional as the launch partner through a rigorous evaluation matrix scoring candidates on transaction volume (40% weight, targeting >$500M B2B GMV), scale potential (30%), integration readiness (15%), partnership willingness (10%), and risk fit (5%). Wayfair scored 92/100 against Grainger, Amazon Business, Overstock Pro, and HD Supply.

I developed the financial model: $1.6M Year 1 revenue for Capital One (transaction fees + platform fees + risk insurance), breakeven by mid-Year 2, and $7M+ three-year cumulative contribution. For Wayfair, net benefit exceeded $528M Year 1.

The 5-month implementation was structured in phases: Foundation (infrastructure, data pipelines, monitoring), Risk Model Development (feature engineering on 500K+ historical records, model training with AUC-ROC >0.82), Core API Development (Credit Decision, Risk Monitoring, Credit Management, Reporting APIs — all meeting sub-100ms latency targets), Integration with Wayfair systems (checkout, data streams, security hardening), and Graduated Rollout (pilot with subset, parallel run, 50% then 100% traffic ramp over two weeks).

### Architecture

API-first architecture with four core services: Credit Decision API (real-time scoring), Risk Monitoring API (batch jobs + alerting), Credit Management API (limit adjustments), and Reporting API (audit and finance dashboards). The risk model combined interpretable Logistic Regression (for regulatory explainability) with Gradient Boosted Machine ensemble (for accuracy). Feature store aggregated real-time signals from Wayfair's order and payment Kafka streams, Dun & Bradstreet business bureau data (monthly refresh), and internal trade payment history. The explainability engine generated human-readable reason codes for every credit decision to satisfy FCRA requirements.

| Metric | Result |
|--------|--------|
| Conversion rate lift | 8–12% increase in completed B2B orders |
| Default reduction | 22% reduction through predictive monitoring |
| Manual review reduction | 95% fewer manual credit reviews |
| API latency | Sub-100ms (target met) |
| Year 1 benefit (Wayfair) | $529M+ (revenue + loss reduction) |

## Project 3: Galderma Supply Chain Visibility Platform (SCOUT)

**Enterprise Supply Chain · Pharma · Oracle Fusion**

### Overview

Launched a unified supply chain analytics and reconciliation module for Galderma, a $3.2 billion global pharmaceutical company operating in 100+ countries. The platform resolved critical data fragmentation across WMS, TMS, Oracle E-Business Suite, 3PL partner systems, and the new Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP following a major ERP modernization. Delivered 292% ROI ($3.5M in quantified business benefit).

### The Problem

Galderma's ERP modernization consolidated the system of record onto Oracle Fusion Cloud ERP. However, the company could not replace downstream systems: warehouse management systems were contractually entrenched with 3PL partners ($50M+ contracts), Oracle E-Business Suite had a migration scheduled for Year 2, and Synergy (the 3PL logistics platform) was partner-managed with no direct control.

This created what I called the "80/20 problem": 80% of supply chain data lived in systems Galderma did not fully control. The consequences were severe. ASN (Advanced Shipping Notice) mismatches delayed revenue recognition by 2–3% quarterly. Inventory discrepancies between systems inflated working capital (5–10% DSO/DIO swing). Regulatory traceability gaps (drug lot tracking, cold-chain documentation) risked $500K+ in remediation if flagged by FDA or EMA auditors. Service level SLA failures (2-day delivery to providers) cascaded to 3–5% annual revenue leakage.

### My Process

I conducted 30+ user interviews across supply chain operations, finance, quality assurance, and IT. I also analyzed 500+ support tickets to identify recurring failure patterns. Root cause analysis revealed three structural problems: data model fragmentation (same fields named differently across systems), process gaps (no automated reconciliation between Oracle Fusion and downstream systems), and visibility gaps (no single dashboard showing end-to-end supply chain health).

I evaluated four options: maintain manual Excel reconciliation (rejected), deploy point tools (rejected), build standalone custom platform (rejected), or build an orchestration layer over existing systems (selected). The key insight: integration over replacement.

I designed a 4-phase, 12-month delivery roadmap. Phase 1: Foundation and ASN Health Dashboard ($250K, 4 months). Phase 2: Inventory Reconciliation Engine ($350K, 4 months). Phase 3: Financial Reconciliation Module ($280K, 3 months). Phase 4: Audit and Governance Layer ($120K, 1 month). I allocated 20% of the total budget to change management because the finance team needed visible early wins by Month 3 to maintain executive sponsorship.

### Architecture

The platform was built as an orchestration layer with a canonical data model that normalized fields across all source systems. Inbound connectors pulled data from Oracle Fusion (real-time API), WMS (SFTP batch), EBS (database replication), and Synergy (API + file drops). A reconciliation engine performed automated matching with configurable tolerance thresholds. The analytics layer was powered by Power BI dashboards tracking ASN health, inventory discrepancies, financial reconciliation status, and regulatory traceability. Event tracking infrastructure enabled real-time alerting for anomalies.

## Project 4: Prism Health Grant Management & Compliance Platform

**Internal Enterprise SaaS · Healthcare Nonprofit · HIPAA**

### Overview

Built an integrated grant management platform for Prism Health North Texas, a $150M nonprofit healthcare organization operating 12 community health clinics and 8 social service programs. The platform unified fragmented grant data across finance, HR, program operations, and compliance systems, replacing spreadsheet-based tracking with automated allocation, real-time budget monitoring, one-click funder reports, and full HIPAA compliance.

### The Problem

Prism Health's revenue structure was 88% grant-dependent: 42% federal grants (HRSA, CDC, SAMHSA, HHS), 28% state grants (Texas Health & Human Services Commission), and 18% private foundations. This meant no grants equaled no operations — if major federal awards failed to renew, specific clinics would close within 90 days. Staff layoffs were grant-tied: each grant funded 15–50 FTE positions with no fungible budget.

The organization managed 47 active grants across 15+ funders, each with unique allowable cost rules, staff eligibility restrictions, unit restrictions, outcome reporting requirements, and audit schedules. A real example: one HRSA grant ($2.1M) funded primary care at the downtown clinic but explicitly prohibited use for mental health staff salaries, indirect costs above 8%, care in the suburban clinic, and equipment purchases over $5K. If Prism accidentally charged $80K of mental health coordinator salary to this grant, the entire award could be audited, resulting in repayment demands and future ineligibility.

Before the platform, the CFO had no real-time visibility into grant spending. The compliance officer spent three weeks per audit pulling together spreadsheets, emails, and folder structures. Grant renewal rate was only 68%. The quantified annual risk exposure was $1.2M+ in potential audit findings, clawbacks, and lost renewals.

### My Process

I structured discovery into four parallel workstreams over 6 weeks. Total effort: 42 stakeholder interviews, 28 hours of process observations, and synthesis workshops with cross-functional teams. I evaluated three scenarios: Do Nothing ($1.2M annual risk), Point Solutions ($400K investment with gaps), and Integrated Platform ($1M investment, projected $3.86M ROI). The integrated platform was selected.

The core strategy was an "Allocation Orchestration Platform" — not replacing Sage Intacct but orchestrating data from Sage, Azure AD, and program operations into a unified view with automated compliance checks. I authored 8+ HIPAA-compliant PRDs, established a 140-row compliance framework matrix, and delivered all releases on time with zero security incidents.

### Architecture

Three-tier architecture deployed on Microsoft Azure. Frontend: React 18 + TypeScript SPA with Material-UI, Redux Toolkit. Backend: Node.js 18 LTS with Express, Prisma ORM, REST API with JWT via Azure AD. Data tier: Azure SQL with geo-redundant backups and 7-year audit retention. Integration: Azure Functions for Sage Intacct data pulls, Azure AD for SSO and RBAC. Total Azure spend: under $50K annually.

**B2B Healthcare SaaS · AI/ML · Patient Experience**

## Article 1: From API Spec to PRD — How I Translate Technical Capabilities Into Product Requirements

**Category: Framework · Reading time: 12 minutes**

Most PRDs start with user stories. Mine start with the API surface.

This might sound backwards. Conventional product management wisdom says you begin with the user, understand their pain, and then work with engineering to figure out what's technically possible. And in many contexts — consumer apps, early-stage startups, design-led organizations — that's exactly right.

But in enterprise B2B, the API is the product. When I was building Centene's care coordination platform, my "users" were nonprofit organizations whose case workers would interact with our system through a web portal — but the actual value delivery happened through APIs. Every referral created, every member profile accessed, every outcome recorded, every audit trail generated was an API call. The portal was a thin presentation layer. The APIs were the business logic, the compliance engine, the integration layer, and the data pipeline all in one.

So I wrote 50+ pages of OpenAPI 3.0 specifications before writing a single user story. Here's why that worked, and how you can apply the same framework.

### The Framework: Problem → Capability → Data Model → API → PRD

**Step 1:** Start with the problem statement, not the solution. In Centene's case, we had four problems: fragmented information access, manual unstructured coordination, inability to measure outcomes, and compliance risk. Each problem was quantified. Fragmented information cost $937,500 per year in wasted labor across the partner network.

**Step 2:** Translate each problem into a technical capability. Fragmented information becomes "real-time member context." Manual coordination becomes "structured workflow and tasks." Unmeasured outcomes becomes "automated outcome tracking and reporting." Compliance risk becomes "role-based access and audit."

**Step 3:** Design the data model for each capability. What entities exist? What relationships between them? What fields does each entity need? For the referral workflow capability, the entities were: Referral (with type, urgency, status, SLA), Task (auto-generated from referral templates), Note (case worker updates), and Notification (SLA alerts). The status field had explicit states: PENDING → ACCEPTED → IN_PROGRESS → COMPLETED/FAILED. Each state transition had business rules and SLA timers attached.

**Step 4:** Define the API surface. For each entity and workflow, what endpoints are needed? POST /referrals to create a referral. PUT /referrals/{id}/acknowledge for the nonprofit to accept. POST /referrals/{id}/notes for case worker updates. PUT /tasks/{id}/status for task progression. POST /referrals/{id}/outcomes for outcome recording. Each endpoint has a request schema, response schema, authentication requirements, rate limits, and error codes.

**Step 5:** Now write the PRD. At this point, the PRD almost writes itself. Each user story maps directly to an API endpoint. "As a case worker, I want to see a member's risk score and care plan when I open their profile, so I can begin intervention immediately without manual research." That's GET /members/{id}/context. The acceptance criteria are the API response schema: risk score present, care plan present, eligibility status confirmed, comorbidities listed. Testable, measurable, unambiguous.

### Why This Works Better Than User Stories First

**Clarity for engineering.** When I hand engineers an API spec, there is zero ambiguity about what to build. The data model is defined. The endpoints are defined. The business rules are embedded in the status transitions and validation rules.

**Testability from day one.** Every acceptance criterion maps to an API call with an expected response. QA can write test cases before a single line of code is written.

**Compliance built in.** In regulated industries, you need to prove that every data access is authorized, logged, and auditable. By designing the API with RBAC, data masking, and audit logging from the start, compliance isn't bolted on at the end.

**Reusability.** The same APIs that power the web portal also power the mobile app (added in Phase 2) and can power future integrations. Because the business logic lives in the API layer, not the presentation layer, any new interface is just a new frontend consuming the same endpoints.

### When Not to Use This Approach

This framework works best for enterprise B2B platforms, internal tools, and API-first products. It is not the right approach for consumer apps where the interaction design is the product, early-stage products where you're still validating the problem, or products where the UX is the primary challenge.

But if you're building something where the data model matters more than the pixel layout, where compliance is not optional, and where multiple systems need to interoperate — start with the API. The PRD will follow.

## Article 2: RICE Is Not Enough — Why I Add Compliance Risk Scoring to Every Prioritization Matrix

**Category: Prioritization · Reading time: 10 minutes**

Standard RICE scoring works brilliantly for consumer product backlogs. But the first time I used RICE in a regulated industry, it nearly caused a catastrophe.

At Prism Health, I inherited a backlog of 200+ items. I RICE-scored them all. A HIPAA audit logging enhancement scored low: small Reach (only compliance officers), moderate Impact, high Confidence, medium Effort. It ranked 47th. Meanwhile, a UI improvement for the grant dashboard scored 3rd.

Two months later, an external auditor flagged our logging gaps. The remediation cost us 3 weeks of engineering time, $45,000 in emergency consulting fees, and a conditional finding that put $800,000 in future funding at risk. The UI improvement we shipped instead? It saved users about 12 minutes per week.

That experience taught me that RICE has a blind spot: it does not account for the asymmetric downside risk of not doing something.

### The Extended Framework: RICE-CR

I now use RICE with a Compliance Risk multiplier:

**Priority Score = (Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort) × Compliance Risk Multiplier**

The Compliance Risk Multiplier is scored 1–5: 1 = no compliance implications. 2 = minor (internal policy). 3 = moderate (could trigger audit observation). 4 = significant (audit finding, financial penalty). 5 = critical (funding clawback, enforcement, shutdown).

The key insight: items scoring 4 or 5 go to the top of the backlog regardless of RICE score. These are compliance obligations, not feature choices.

### How to Score Compliance Risk

Ask for each backlog item: What regulation does this address? What's the consequence of non-compliance (in dollars)? When is the next audit? Is there a compensating control? Has this gap been previously flagged?

### Applying RICE-CR

The HIPAA logging enhancement under RICE-CR: Base score 3.0 × CR Multiplier 5 = 15.0. Rank: 2nd (instead of 47th). That one adjustment would have prevented 3 weeks of emergency remediation and $45,000 in consulting fees.

Use RICE-CR in any regulated environment: healthcare, fintech, government, pharma, education. Do not use it for consumer apps or pre-revenue startups where regulatory compliance is not material.

## Article 3: Running A/B Tests in Regulated Industries — The Compliance-First Experimentation Playbook

**Category: Experimentation · Reading time: 14 minutes**

At Capital One, every A/B test on the credit decisioning platform had to pass Fair Lending review. At Centene, patient safety constraints meant I could not randomly assign care interventions. At Galderma, FDA regulations governed testing changes to drug traceability workflows.

The core principle: compliance is not the enemy of experimentation. It is a design constraint that forces you to run better experiments.

### Step 1: Classify Your Experiment's Regulatory Exposure

**Tier 1 — No exposure.** Button colors, email subject lines, welcome messages. Run like any standard experiment.

**Tier 2 — Indirect exposure.** Touches a regulated system, but the variable itself isn't regulated. Dashboard layout changes, onboarding step sequences. Requires compliance review to confirm no inadvertent impact.

**Tier 3 — Direct exposure.** Changes a regulated variable: credit thresholds (FCRA), health data display (HIPAA), drug traceability triggers (FDA). Requires full compliance review, legal sign-off, pre-registered hypotheses, and defined rollback criteria.

### Step 2: Design for Auditability

Maintain an "Experiment Passport" for each test: written hypothesis, documented design, compliance sign-off, implementation documentation, results documentation, and decision record. This single artifact satisfies auditor requirements.

### Step 3: Use Feature Flags

Feature flags are non-negotiable: instant rollback, audit trail, graduated rollout, and targeted exposure. At Centene, I used flags to expose treatments to specific nonprofit tiers and roll back instantly if needed.

### Step 4: Define Guardrail Metrics

Every experiment has guardrail metrics that are often more important than the primary metric. At Capital One, guardrails included: default rate, Fair Lending compliance, explainability compliance, and latency. If any guardrail breached its threshold, the experiment auto-paused.

### Step 5: Run Compliance Review in Parallel

Don't wait for formal review. Share drafts with compliance as you design. By formal submission, 80% of concerns are already addressed. Frame experiments as compliance tools: "This test demonstrates our SLA enforcement is working."

## Article 4: Why Your B2B Discovery Process Is Broken (And How Ride-Alongs Fixed Mine)

**Category: Strategy · Reading time: 11 minutes**

Surveys tell you what users say. Ride-alongs tell you what they do.

At Centene, I spent 3 days shadowing nonprofit case workers. I watched a case worker toggle between 6 browser tabs, manually copy member data from emails into spreadsheets, and wait 12 minutes on hold to ask if a member was still enrolled. None of this appeared in interviews. When asked about pain points, case workers said "we need more training" and "better communication with Centene." Those are symptoms, not root causes. The root cause — information asymmetry — only became visible through direct observation.

### Why B2B Discovery Is Harder Than B2C

In B2C, the product interaction is the entire scope. In B2B, the product is one tool in a complex workflow spanning multiple systems, teams, and organizational boundaries. A case worker's "journey" includes receiving emails, opening spreadsheets, making calls, checking systems, and writing notes — then maybe your platform. If you only study the platform interaction, you miss 80% of the workflow.

Users are not aware of their own workarounds. The case worker spending 1.5 hours per member on manual research describes it as "doing her job," not "1.5 hours of waste."

### How to Run an Effective Ride-Along

**Permission and trust.** Work through leadership. Explain you're observing workflows, not evaluating performance.

**Observe, don't intervene.** The hardest part is keeping your mouth shut. Take notes. Record timestamps. Count steps. Ask "what happens next?" not "what do you wish happened?"

**Process mapping in real time.** Track: Task (what), Tool (what system), Time (how long), Friction (what slowed them), Workaround (how they coped).

**Quantify everything.** Convert observations to numbers: 1.5 hours × 500 referrals × $25/hour = $18,750/year/nonprofit. That number anchored the business case.

### When Not Feasible

Use virtual ride-alongs: screen-sharing sessions where users work on their actual systems while you observe. Less rich than in-person but far better than interviews alone. Key: observe first, ask later.

## Article 6: How I Built a $3.5M Business Case That Got Funded in Two Weeks

**Category: Business Case · Reading time: 11 minutes**

At Galderma, the supply chain was leaking money. Everyone knew it. Nobody could prove it. I spent 40 hours learning the domain, then built a business case that connected every feature to a P&L line item. Funded in two weeks.

### Step 1: Frame as Risk Mitigation, Not IT Upgrade

Never say "we need a reconciliation module." Say: "We're recognizing revenue 2–3% late quarterly because ASN mismatches delay receipt posting. Working capital is inflated 5–10%. Top-down estimates don't survive scrutiny. For every line item, show the calculation: ASN mismatch rate (12%) × average shipment value ($45K) × monthly volume (200) × average delay (8 days) = $1.08M quarterly in delayed recognition. Every number has a source.

### Step 3: Phase the Investment to De-Risk

$1M over 12 months sounds risky. Four phases of $250K–$350K with milestones between each sounds prudent. Phase 1: ASN Dashboard (the visible early win). Phase 2: Inventory Reconciliation. Phase 3: Financial Reconciliation. Phase 4: Audit & Governance. Each phase delivers standalone value.

### Step 4: Allocate Budget for Change Management

20% of budget ($200K). Training, documentation, super-user programs, executive communication cadence. Phase 1 was designed to deliver the dashboard first — not the most technically impactful deliverable, but the most politically important one.

### The Result

Full $1M approved in one meeting. 292% ROI within 12 months. Phased approach caught issues early. Change management ensured adoption.

The lesson: business cases are about connecting features to financial outcomes, phasing to de-risk, and making the first deliverable politically visible.

# SECTION 11: AI AGENTS

Three autonomous AI agent prototypes for end-to-end PM task automation.

---

## Agent 1: Discovery Automation Agent

### Purpose
Ingests unstructured user feedback (support tickets, NPS surveys, Slack messages, app store reviews) and produces structured discovery outputs: clustered pain points, hypothesis statements, and a research plan with interview scripts.

### Why This Matters
At Centene, I manually analyzed 500+ support tickets — 3 days of work. This agent replicates that in 12 minutes with 87% overlap in theme identification, plus discovers themes manual analysis missed.

### Architecture

**Data Ingestion:** Jira API (tickets, last 90 days, customer-reported), NPS survey connector, Slack channel reader, CSV/JSON batch upload.

**Processing Pipeline:**
1. Text Preprocessing — Clean, normalize, remove PII via regex + NER, standardize terminology
2. Embedding & Clustering — sentence-transformers (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) → HDBSCAN clustering
3. Cluster Analysis via Claude — For each cluster, identify: core pain point, severity, frequency, hypothesis statement ("We believe [segment] experiences [pain] because [cause], resulting in [impact]"), two validation research questions
4. Research Plan Generation — Prioritize hypotheses by severity × frequency, recommend methods, generate interview scripts, suggest timeline and sample sizes

**Output:** Discovery Brief (Markdown), Hypothesis Board (JSON for Productboard/Notion import), Interview Scripts, Slack notification with summary.

**Tech Stack:** Python 3.11 · Claude API · sentence-transformers · HDBSCAN · pandas · Jira REST API · Slack SDK · Docker · Cron (weekly)

---

## Agent 2: Prioritization Engine Agent

### Purpose
Ingests product backlog from Jira/Linear/CSV, applies multi-factor scoring (RICE + Compliance Risk + Strategic Alignment + Engineering Complexity + Dependency Score), produces stack-ranked list with full reasoning.

### Why This Matters
At Prism Health, prioritization meetings took 4 hours per sprint for 200+ items. This agent pre-scores everything, surfaces top 20 for human discussion, and flags ambiguous items. Meetings dropped from 4 hours to 45 minutes.

### Architecture

**Scoring Dimensions:**
1. RICE — Claude estimates Reach, Impact, Confidence from description; Effort from story points
2. Compliance Risk (1–5) — Claude checks description against regulatory requirements list
3. Strategic Alignment (1–5) — Comparison to annual roadmap themes
4. Engineering Complexity — Claude assesses from description and linked issues
5. Dependency Score — Maps linked issues; items unblocking 3+ others get multiplier

**Composite:** (RICE × CR Multiplier × Strategic Weight) + Dependency Bonus

**Human Review Flags:** Confidence <50%, CR and RICE conflict, multiple stakeholders with different priorities, ambiguous descriptions.

**Output:** Stack-ranked JSON/CSV, Priority Brief (top 20 with rationale), Notion update, Slack summary.

**Tech Stack:** Python 3.11 · Claude API (structured JSON) · Jira REST API · Linear GraphQL · Notion API · Slack SDK · Docker

---

## Agent 3: Sprint Iteration Agent

### Purpose
Real-time monitoring agent connecting Jira + GitHub. Tracks sprint progress, detects risk signals, generates automated reports (daily standups, mid-sprint health checks, retrospectives), and alerts PM to emerging issues.

### Why This Matters
PMs spend 8–12 hours per sprint on management overhead. This agent automates 80%, freeing PMs for strategy and research.

### Architecture

**Data Sources:** Jira Webhooks (status changes, scope changes, comments), GitHub Webhooks (PRs, reviews, merges, CI/CD), Slack activity (optional sentiment).

**Monitoring (every 2 hours):** Sprint state model tracking stories by status, cumulative vs. planned velocity, scope changes, blocker duration, PR cycle time, contributor load balance.

**Risk Detection:**
- Velocity Risk: <40% complete at midpoint → "Sprint At Risk"
- Scope Creep: >15% above original commitment → alert
- Blocker: >24 hours in blocked status → escalation
- PR Stagnation: >48 hours without review → notify reviewer + PM
- Load Imbalance: >2x average per contributor → redistribution flag

**Automated Reports:**
- Daily Standup (8:30 AM): completed, in progress, blockers, metrics
- Mid-Sprint Health: velocity projection, risks, comparison to 3-sprint trend, scope adjustment suggestions
- Retrospective: velocity analysis, carry-over, blocker root causes, scope changes, 3 improvement experiments

**Tech Stack:** Python 3.11 · Claude API · Jira + GitHub Webhooks · Slack SDK · APScheduler · PostgreSQL · Docker · PagerDuty

# SECTION 12: GOOGLE A2A PROTOCOL SHOWCASES

Three multi-agent systems using Google's Agent2Agent protocol for complex PM handoffs.

**Background:** A2A enables agents to discover capabilities (Agent Cards), exchange tasks (submitted → working → completed/failed), stream updates, and handle errors. For PM, A2A solves the information loss problem at every handoff: discovery → requirements → engineering → QA → launch.

---

## Showcase 1: Discovery → Requirements Handoff Agent

### Problem Solved
Discovery findings (research, pain points, hypotheses) are documented in one tool and manually translated into requirements in another. Translation loses context: user quotes, data evidence, research methodology. When an engineer asks "why is this a requirement?" 3 months later, traceability is broken.

### System Architecture

**Discovery Agent** — Capabilities: process_user_research, generate_pain_point_clusters, produce_hypothesis_statements, publish_discovery_findings. Agent Card exposes these via A2A.

**Requirements Agent** — Capabilities: receive_discovery_findings, generate_requirements, create_user_stories, maintain_traceability.

### A2A Task Flow

1. Discovery Agent completes analysis → publishes task artifact (pain clusters, hypotheses, user quotes, impact estimates)
2. Requirements Agent receives artifact → generates BRs, FRs, NFRs with acceptance criteria, each linked to discovery source
3. Requirements Agent publishes generated requirements as new A2A artifact for downstream agents

### Key Innovation
Full context preservation. FR-012 traces to → hypothesis statement → discovery cluster (11/12 interviewees) → specific user quote. Automatic traceability. No manual documentation.

---

## Showcase 2: Cross-Team Roadmap Synchronization

### Problem Solved
Enterprise teams have interdependent roadmaps tracked in spreadsheets and discussed in meetings that are inevitably miscommunicated. Team A needs Team B's API by Q1 end; Team C needs Team A's feature. Dependencies are discovered too late.

### System Architecture
Each team operates a **Team Agent** with capabilities: roadmap state management (reads from Jira/Linear), dependency declaration (publishes A2A requests to other teams), feasibility assessment (checks requests against capacity and responds: COMMITTED / CONDITIONAL / AT RISK / DECLINED), and conflict detection via a central orchestrator.

### A2A Task Lifecycle

1. Team A's agent sends dependency request: `{ "deliverable": "Member Risk Score Endpoint v2", "needed_by": "2025-06-30", "priority": "HIGH" }`
2. Team B's agent checks capacity, responds: `{ "status": "CONDITIONAL", "estimated_delivery": "2025-07-15", "conditions": ["Requires data eng migration by June 1"] }`
3. Orchestrator detects conflict (needed June 30, estimated July 15) → generates alert with options (accelerate, delay, or find alternative) → sends to both PMs via Slack

### Key Innovation
Continuous synchronization. When Team B's velocity drops and delivery shifts from July 15 to August 1, the orchestrator immediately alerts all affected teams — no surprise discovery at next quarterly review. Handles 5 concurrent team agents.

---

## Showcase 3: Launch Coordination Orchestrator

### Problem Solved
Enterprise launches require 15+ handoffs: QA, compliance, security, docs, training, comms, monitoring, rollback, executive approval, deployment. Currently managed via spreadsheets, Slack, and meetings. PM spends 80+ hours over 2 weeks on logistics.

### System Architecture

**Orchestrator** manages a DAG (directed acyclic graph) of tasks. **Specialist Agents:**
- QA Agent: test execution, coverage, sign-off
- Compliance Agent: regulatory review, audit trail, clearance
- Documentation Agent: user docs, API docs, training
- Monitoring Agent: dashboards, alerts, runbooks
- Communication Agent: customer notifications, internal announcements
- Deployment Agent: feature flags, traffic ramping, health checks, rollback

### A2A Task DAG

**Phase 1 — Pre-Launch (Parallel):** QA, Compliance, Documentation, Monitoring run simultaneously. Complete when all 4 succeed.

**Phase 2 — Approval Gate (Sequential):** Compliance clearance → executive approval (human-in-the-loop via Slack). No deployment without both.

**Phase 3 — Deployment (Graduated):** Enable flags at 10% → monitor 2 hours → 50% → monitor 4 hours → 100% → customer notification. Unhealthy at any stage → auto-rollback + incident notification.

**Phase 4 — Post-Launch (Parallel):** 24-hour health report, release notes, success announcement, archive for audit.

### Streaming Updates
All agents report via A2A streaming: `{ "agent": "QA Agent", "status": "working", "progress": "847/1200 tests (70.6%)", "eta": "45 minutes" }`

### Key Innovation
Compliance gates as first-class DAG nodes — deployment architecturally cannot proceed without clearance. Full audit trail satisfies HIPAA, FCRA, FDA change management requirements. Reduces launch coordination from 80+ hours over 2 weeks to 20 hours over 3 days.

**Tech Stack (All A2A):** Python 3.11 · Google A2A Protocol SDK · Claude API · Jira/GitHub APIs · Slack SDK · PagerDuty · PostgreSQL · Redis · Docker · Apache Airflow · FastAPI (Agent Cards)
