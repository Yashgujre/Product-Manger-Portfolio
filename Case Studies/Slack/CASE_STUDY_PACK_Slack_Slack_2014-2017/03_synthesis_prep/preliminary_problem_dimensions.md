# Preliminary Problem Dimensions

_Candidate problem dimensions based on evidence gathered to date. Facts only — no framing or interpretation. Interpretation is reserved for Skill #2 (Problem Definition)._
_These are groupings of related friction signals, not conclusions._

---

## Dimension 1 — Communication Fragmentation
**Pain label:** Work conversations, files, and tool updates scattered across email, IM, project tools, and file stores with no unified view.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Each tool addressed one workflow slice; no single layer aggregated cross-tool communication.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #3: Dropbox, Google Docs, GitHub, Asana used separately (Source C-1)
- Friction #2: "Email bankruptcy" framing (Source C-1, D-1)
- Friction #4: Problem "under everyone's noses" — market conditioned to accept fragmentation (Source C-2)
- Launch value prop: "port in conversations and links to other work from dozens of other apps" (Source C-1)

---

## Dimension 2 — Email Overload as Productivity Tax
**Pain label:** Email as primary collaboration medium consumed ~28% of knowledge workers' workweeks, with measurable productivity cost.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Email was designed for asynchronous broadcast, not real-time team coordination; teams repurposed it for collaboration despite poor fit.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #1: 28% of workweek on email (benchmarks.csv — McKinsey/Forbes 2014)
- Friction #16: 20–25% productivity lift possible from social/collaboration tools (benchmarks.csv — McKinsey 2012)
- Friction #2: "Email bankruptcy" (multiple sources)
- Butterfield: "everyone will be using" centralized communication within 10 years (Source C-2)

---

## Dimension 3 — Activation Gap (Non-Adoption ≠ Non-Interest)
**Pain label:** Teams that adopted Slack but did not cross a behavioral threshold (~2,000 messages) showed weak retention — the activation moment was real but not guaranteed.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Product value not immediately self-evident; required team-level critical mass of usage before the collaboration network effect materialized.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #15: 2,000-message activation threshold (Source C-3 — retrospective; primary source needed)
- Beta day-1 demand: 8,000 requests → 15,000 within a week despite product being pre-launch (Source C-3)
- "Every single team member every day" during beta — activation when it worked (Source C-1)

---

## Dimension 4 — Scale-Induced Noise Problem
**Pain label:** As team size or Slack usage grew, the product that solved fragmentation introduced a new problem: notification overload, channel chaos, and information overload.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Channels and notifications designed for small-team transparency; no built-in governance layer for large-org deployment.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #5: Notification overload / deep-work disruption (review_themes.md)
- Friction #6: "Too many channels" at large-team scale (review_themes.md)
- Friction #9: Cluttered threads (review_themes.md)
- Friction #13: SMB-to-enterprise governance gap (review_themes.md)

---

## Dimension 5 — Enterprise Adoption Blocker (IT + Security)
**Pain label:** IT teams and procurement blocked or delayed Slack adoption due to security, compliance, and governance concerns — particularly in regulated industries.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Consumer-grade SaaS product entering enterprise with insufficient audit, SSO, and data-residency controls for enterprise IT requirements.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #11: IT governance concern — external chat app without compliance controls (review_themes.md)
- Friction #13: SMB-to-enterprise governance gap (review_themes.md)
- IBM 2016 institutional adoption: required Slack to become "officially supported" before enterprise-wide rollout possible (Source D-6)
- Enterprise Grid development (post-2017) as product response to this dimension

---

## Dimension 6 — PLG-to-Enterprise Transition Friction
**Pain label:** Bottom-up, team-level adoption was Slack's growth engine, but converting viral SMB usage into large enterprise contracts required an entirely different motion.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** PLG motion generated awareness and usage; enterprise deals required top-down security, legal, and procurement approval that the PLG motion did not address.
**Evidence references:**
- S-1: "small teams adopting bottom-up and later expanding into departmental and enterprise-wide deployments" (investor_excerpts.md)
- NRR of 143%: expansion within existing customers (investor_excerpts.md)
- IBM deal: 2016 institutional adoption after years of ad-hoc usage (Source D-6)
- Friction #12: Cost/feature gating slowed expansion beyond free tier (review_themes.md)
- Friction #10: Adoption requires admin governance that SMB users don't have (review_themes.md)

## Dimension 7 — Incumbent GTM Lock-In (Competitive Friction)
**Pain label:** Existing tools (HipChat, Yammer) required top-down IT approval and paid procurement before teams could even try them — creating a structural adoption barrier that Slack's freemium model was designed to bypass.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Enterprise software distribution norms assumed IT-led evaluation and purchase; no incumbent offered a zero-friction, team-initiated trial path at this scale.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #18: HipChat paid-first GTM — purchase required before embedding (Source G-1, G-3)
- Friction #19: Market conditioned to IT-approved, procurement-gated tooling (Source G-1, G-5)
- Zapier data: Slack overtook HipChat in September 2014 — within 7 months of public launch (Source G-3)
- Butterfield: Slack embeds organically and then scales into enterprise (Source G-8)

---

## Dimension 8 — Competitive Market Transition Risk (End of Window)
**Pain label:** By late 2016, Slack faced a materially different competitive environment than 2014 — Microsoft entered with bundled Teams (135M subscriber base), collapsing the freemium price advantage that had powered Slack's growth.
**Suspected root cause (from evidence):** Slack's PLG moat was built on freemium + integrations + UX; Teams negated the freemium cost argument by bundling at $0 marginal cost within existing Office 365 contracts.
**Evidence references:**
- Friction #21: Microsoft Teams Nov 2016 preview / Mar 2017 GA with 135M Office 365 distribution (Source G-7)
- Microsoft considered acquiring Slack for $8B before deciding to build Teams — confirming Slack's market-leader position at end of window (Source G-7)
- HipChat already losing: overtaken by Slack Sept 2014, acquired and shut down 2018 (Source G-2, G-3)
- Yammer: broadcast social, not real-time chat — adjacent rather than direct (Source G-6)
